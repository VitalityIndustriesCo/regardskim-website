# RegardsKim Brand Direction

## Brand Core

**Brand name:** RegardsKim  
**Sign-off / brand phrase:** Kind regards, Kim  
**Positioning:** Post-purchase support for Shopify stores  
**Tagline:** **Shopify helps them buy. Kim helps them stay.**

RegardsKim should feel like the most capable person in the back office: warm, efficient, organised, and endlessly polite. The retro influence gives the brand memorability and charm, but the product itself should still feel current, fast, and trustworthy. This is not nostalgia for nostalgia's sake. It is a visual metaphor for dependable admin excellence.

The emotional promise is simple: **your customer emails are handled, your brand feels cared for, and you stop drowning in inbox work.**

---

## 1. Visual Style

### Creative Direction
The visual world should be rooted in **1950s commercial illustration** and **mid-century office culture**, translated into a polished SaaS brand.

Think:
- vintage female office worker / secretary / personal assistant energy
- rotary-phone-era competence, but with modern ecommerce workflows
- cheerful mid-century advertising layouts
- hand-drawn character illustration paired with clean contemporary web structure
- premium editorial restraint rather than diner-kitsch novelty

This should feel closer to:
- a beautifully art-directed vintage print ad
- a boutique stationery brand
- a stylish front desk at a great hotel

Not closer to:
- novelty Americana
- rockabilly costume branding
- retro parody
- chaotic diner signage or loud comic-book camp

### Visual Keywords
- polished
- capable
- charming
- organised
- warm
- editorial
- nostalgic
- trustworthy
- feminine without being delicate
- premium but approachable

## 2. Color Palette

The current warm coral direction should stay central and be expanded into a full mid-century-inspired system.

### Primary Palette

**1. Warm Coral — Primary Brand Color**  
Use for key buttons, highlights, hero accents, logo details, illustrations.  
Suggested range: `#E97C6B` to `#F08E7F`

**2. Cream — Primary Background**  
Soft paper-like neutral for most page backgrounds.  
Suggested: `#F8F1E7`

**3. Ink Navy — Primary Text / Contrast**  
For headings, body text, UI contrast, and sophistication.  
Suggested: `#233547`

### Secondary Palette

**4. Dusty Rose**  
For secondary accents, cards, hover states.  
Suggested: `#D8A6A0`

**5. Muted Teal**  
A balancing mid-century accent for diagrams, badges, and small UI moments.  
Suggested: `#7FA7A1`

**6. Butter Gold**  
For highlights, stars, callouts, micro-illustration details.  
Suggested: `#E5BE6B`

### Support Neutrals

**7. Warm Taupe**  
Borders, dividers, shadows, subtle texture overlays.  
Suggested: `#B79E8E`

**8. Soft Peach**  
Alternate section fills, testimonials, pricing backgrounds.  
Suggested: `#F6DDD2`

**9. Off-White Paper**  
For cards and elevated surfaces.  
Suggested: `#FFF9F3`

### Color Behavior
- Coral is the brand's recognisable signal color.
- Cream and off-white keep the retro look elevated and breathable.
- Navy prevents the palette from becoming too sugary or gimmicky.
- Teal and gold should be used sparingly, like art-direction seasoning.
- Avoid bright pure red, neon pink, harsh black, and highly saturated blues.

## 3. Typography Suggestions

The typography should balance **mid-century personality** with **modern SaaS clarity**.

### Primary Heading Direction
Choose a heading font with retro softness, confident curves, and poster-like charm.

Suggested directions:
- **Recoleta** — warm, stylish, retro-inspired serif with personality
- **Canela** — elegant, premium, editorial, less overtly retro
- **Cooper-style modern revival** — use carefully for larger display moments only
- **Fraunces** — variable serif that can lean vintage while staying sharp

**Use case:** hero headings, section headers, pricing titles, major pull quotes

### Body Font Direction
The body copy should remain highly readable and modern.

Suggested directions:
- **Inter**
- **Manrope**
- **Avenir Next**
- **Söhne**
- **Work Sans**

**Use case:** body text, FAQs, buttons, UI labels, navigation

### Accent / Utility Typography
A small secondary accent font can reinforce the retro stationery feel.

Suggested directions:
- clean all-caps grotesk for labels and UI metadata
- subtle script influence only in tiny doses, if at all

Avoid overly decorative script fonts for core branding. The sign-off “Kind regards, Kim” should feel elegant and human, not wedding-invitation ornate.

### Typography System Recommendation
- **Display:** Recoleta or Fraunces
- **Body/UI:** Inter or Avenir Next
- **Small labels:** Inter SemiBold / all caps with tracking

### Typography Tone Rules
- Keep headings concise and confident.
- Let serif type create warmth; do not over-style everything.
- Avoid cluttered type lockups and novelty retro fonts in body copy.

## 4. Illustration Style

### Core Illustration Approach
Illustration should feel like **1950s magazine and advertising character art**, simplified for digital product use.

Key traits:
- clean linework
- slightly exaggerated but tasteful facial expressions
- flat or lightly shaded shapes
- limited color palette
- smooth geometric forms
- soft paper texture overlays
- subtle halftone or grain in backgrounds, not on text-heavy UI

### Character Illustration Style
Kim should be the visual anchor of the brand. She appears in:
- hero sections
- onboarding illustrations
- feature explainers
- empty states
- email / support themed graphics
- subtle badges or icons

Her poses should show:
- pointing to inboxes or drafts
- sorting mail
- answering a telephone
- stamping “handled”
- organising a stack of customer requests
- presenting a tidy dashboard

### Iconography
Icons should echo classic office objects, simplified into modern UI symbols:
- envelope
- paper tray
- stamp mark
- filing tab
- desk bell
- tracking arrow
- return box
- reply note

Use rounded corners and mid-century geometry. Avoid generic corporate outline icon sets.

## 5. Kim as a Character

## Who Kim Is Visually
Kim is a 1950s-inspired office professional reimagined for a modern Shopify business. She is not a pin-up, not a mascot joke, and not a cartoon cliché. She is the embodiment of competent customer care.

### Visual Character Notes
- female office worker / PA / secretary silhouette
- neat mid-century hairstyle: rolled bob, tidy wave, or soft flipped ends
- polished but simple makeup styling, if any
- warm, expressive eyes
- friendly, composed smile
- classic blouse, neck scarf, cardigan, or office dress
- headset, telephone, envelope, clipboard, or inbox tray as recurring props
- posture communicates readiness and reliability

### Personality Through Design
Kim should look:
- calm under pressure
- impossibly organised
- quick without feeling rushed
- thoughtful and polite
- reassuring to merchants
- kind to customers
- capable enough to trust with your brand voice

She is the person who never lets an email sit too long, never loses the thread, and always knows where the order is.

### What to Avoid in Character Design
- over-sexualised vintage styling
- exaggerated “housewife” tropes
- caricatured retro clichés
- childish mascot energy
- robotic or sci-fi details
- anything that makes her feel gimmicky or unserious

### Kim's Brand Role
Kim is both:
1. **a character** who gives the brand memorability and emotional warmth
2. **a shorthand for the job being done** — your post-purchase support, handled with care

She should feel like a trusted extension of the merchant's business.

## 6. Tone of Voice for the Website

### Voice Summary
The voice should be:
- friendly
- sharp
- reassuring
- lightly witty
- polished
- clear and benefit-led

This is not a hype-heavy tech product voice. It should sound like a very good operator with taste.

### Tone Characteristics

**1. Competent first**  
The copy should make merchants feel relief. It should sound like the inbox is under control.

**2. Warm, not gushy**  
Friendly language is welcome, but avoid startup fluff and over-excitement.

**3. Retro-charming, not roleplay**  
A few well-placed phrases can nod to the brand world, but the website should not sound like a themed diner menu.

**4. Outcome-focused**  
Talk about fewer customer emails to manage, faster replies, organised drafts, saved time, lower cost.

**5. Plain-English product language**  
Avoid jargon like “AI agent,” “autonomous workflows,” “multi-modal orchestration,” or “LLM-powered”.

### Voice Examples
Good:
- Post-purchase emails, handled.
- Kim reads the inbox, prepares the reply, and keeps things moving.
- Give your customers a prompt, polished response without hiring another pair of hands.

Avoid:
- Deploy an AI support agent to autonomously resolve CX tickets.
- Revolutionary automation for next-generation ecommerce support.
- Your retro support bestie is here to slay the inbox.

### Approved Brand Phrases
- Shopify helps them buy. Kim helps them stay.
- Kind regards, Kim.
- Post-purchase emails, handled.
- Your customer support desk, minus the extra payroll.
- Polite, prompt, and ready in draft.

## 7. Translating the Retro Theme to Web Design

The website should blend **mid-century styling cues** with a clean modern SaaS layout.

### Layout Approach
- generous whitespace
- strong editorial sectioning
- rounded cards and framed content blocks
- subtle asymmetry in illustration placement
- clear hierarchy and modern conversion structure

### Retro Elements to Use
- paper textures on large background areas
- halftone dots in illustration backdrops
- scalloped badges or seal shapes for pricing/value callouts
- postage stamp or airmail stripe motifs in dividers or footers
- rounded pill buttons with strong contrast
- filing-tab inspired section labels
- stationery-like lines, seals, or stamp graphics

### UI Style Notes
- Buttons: rounded, tactile, coral-forward, slightly inset or shadowed
- Cards: cream or off-white with soft borders and minimal drop shadow
- Inputs: simple, clean, not overly skeuomorphic
- Badges: “Handled”, “Ready to send”, “Draft prepared” style labels
- Illustrations should sit alongside screenshots rather than replacing product proof

### Texture Guidance
Use texture sparingly:
- light paper grain on background panels
- subtle print noise in decorative shapes
- no heavy distressed overlays that reduce perceived product quality

The site should still feel premium and software-native. Think **vintage art direction over modern product UX**, not “old website.”

## 8. Photography / Product Image Direction

Even with strong illustration, the website will need product trust signals.

### Product Screenshots
Screenshots should:
- use the coral dashboard theme consistently
- be framed in cream or off-white containers
- be paired with Kim illustrations or office props
- show inbox triage, draft replies, and approval states clearly

### Supporting Imagery
If photography is used at all, prefer:
- close crops of vintage office ephemera
- desk objects like paper trays, stamps, envelopes, pens
- tactile materials such as textured paper and labels

Avoid stock photography of customer support teams in headsets.

## 10. Logo Concepts

### Concept 1: Script Signature + Envelope Mark
**Idea:** A refined wordmark where “Regards” is set simply and “Kim” feels like a confident signature, paired with a small envelope or folded-letter icon.

**Why it works:**
- leans into “Kind regards, Kim” as the emotional brand asset
- feels personal and premium
- easy to use across web, email, and app contexts

**Visual notes:**
- serif or clean sans for “Regards”
- signature-style but legible custom script for “Kim”
- envelope flap or wax-seal-inspired accent in coral
- best for a polished, brand-forward identity

### Concept 2: Retro Secretary Cameo Badge
**Idea:** A circular or scalloped emblem featuring Kim's illustrated profile or bust, paired with the brand name in a mid-century display lockup.

**Why it works:**
- instantly memorable and character-led
- taps directly into the retro office aesthetic
- ideal for social avatars, onboarding, and merchandise

**Visual notes:**
- cameo silhouette or simplified portrait of Kim
- coral, cream, and navy palette
- optional mini ribbon/banner with “Kind regards” or “Handled”
- should remain simple enough to scale down

### Concept 3: Wordmark with Office Desk Symbol
**Idea:** A typographic logo with a custom icon built from an envelope, paper tray, and checkmark/arrow motif — essentially a “handled inbox” symbol with vintage geometry.

**Why it works:**
- strongest option if the brand wants to balance character with software credibility
- clearly communicates support workflow and organisation
- works well in app/product environments

**Visual notes:**
- geometric mid-century icon construction
- soft corners, tidy linework
- coral icon with navy wordmark
- possible hidden “K” shape inside the desk/envelope symbol

### Logo Recommendation
If the goal is strongest differentiation, pursue **Concept 1 or Concept 2** first.
- **Concept 1** is most premium and scalable.
- **Concept 2** is most ownable and memorable.
- **Concept 3** is safest for software flexibility.

## 11. Brand Do / Don't

### Do
- make the brand feel capable and polished
- keep the retro cues art-directed and intentional
- use Kim to build trust and memorability
- speak in clear business outcomes
- pair charm with product proof

### Don't
- make it feel like a novelty costume brand
- lean too hard into kitsch or irony
- overcomplicate the UI with decorative retro effects
- use overt tech jargon in customer-facing materials
- let the character overshadow the utility

**RegardsKim is the charming, impeccably organised post-purchase support desk every Shopify store wishes they had.**
